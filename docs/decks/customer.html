What it is in one sentence

the shortest version

A local accessibility checker that works inside your AI coding tools.

a11y-checker runs on your machine, plugs into Claude Code, Cursor, and Copilot, and reads every interface file your AI touches. The moment the AI writes something that's going to fail an audit, the checker feeds that back into the AI's context so the next edit fixes it.

The code arrives accessible. Not "made accessible later" — accessible the first time it lands in the file.

The problem today the slow loop

how a11y gets checked today

Most accessibility problems are found months after they're written.

Today the loop is slow. A developer writes code and it ships. Weeks or months later an audit runs, finds issues, and files a ticket — back to a developer who has long since moved on to something else. The fix costs more than the original would have, and the same mistakes turn up in the next audit because nothing in the writing moment changed.

The best time to fix an accessibility issue isn't at audit time. It's the second the code is written, while the person — or the AI — writing it still has the whole picture in their head.

Why not a linter two gaps

the tool you already know

Linters can't see the components your team actually ships.

Standard linters work on plain HTML and a fixed set of rules. The moment your code uses your own component library — your buttons, your modals, your design-system pieces — the linter treats those as trusted and moves on. So a real bug, hidden inside a component the linter calls fine, ships untouched.

There's a second gap too. A linter has no sense of what actually matters. A rare edge case and an everyday failure get the same red squiggle, so developers tune the whole thing out. You need real-world signal to know what to prioritize, and a linter doesn't have any.

●04 / 11
What's different the corpus

the difference

Grounded in real audits, not a generic ruleset.

Binclusive runs accessibility audits across real companies. We take what we find, strip every identifying detail, and turn it into a corpus of failure patterns — what tends to break, in what kinds of components, how often.

That means the checker doesn't just say "this is wrong." It says: this is wrong, it's one of the most common ways accessibility breaks on real sites, and here's the fix that worked. A regular linter can't do that — it has never seen a real site, only a rulebook.

In your workflow a fast loop, not a magic fixer

how it feels day to day

Your AI writes the code; the checker corrects course in the same breath.

The setup is a hook plus a small server. When your AI assistant edits an interface file, the checker reads it, finds any accessibility problems, and feeds them straight back into the AI's context as part of the same task. The AI rewrites — usually before you've even reviewed the first version — and what shows up in your diff is already clean.

You still review the diff. The checker isn't a magic fixer; it's a fast loop. But that loop runs hundreds of times a day in the background, on every interface file your team touches, and the result is that the audit-time pile of issues stops growing.

Priority why "common" matters

the magic word

Frequency turns a long list of issues into a short list of priorities.

A first pass on a real site usually surfaces a long list of findings. Most teams look at the list, freeze, and fix the easy ones. The hard ones sit. We've all seen this.

When every finding carries a frequency signal from real audits, the list re-sorts itself. A handful of patterns show up across nearly every site we look at — those are the ones to fix first, because fixing them once removes a class of problems forever. The long tail of rare findings can wait. That's the difference between a list that gets worked and a list that gets ignored.

Privacy local by default

a fair question, answered

Local by default. The corpus is shapes, not code.

Your code never leaves your machine. The checker runs locally — that's not a privacy promise on a marketing page, it's how it's built. There's no upload step, because there's nothing to upload.

A pattern only enters the shared corpus when at least three different companies have hit it independently, with every identifying detail stripped. We learn the shape of the problem; we never learn whose problem it was. That's the rule the system runs on, not the rule we ask you to trust.

●09 / 11
Where it fits the other half of the audit

not a replacement

This is the other half of the audit — not a replacement.

Audits find what already shipped. The checker stops it from shipping. They are two halves of the same job, and you need both. An audit without a write-time tool means the same bugs keep arriving. A write-time tool without audits means nobody is watching the long tail or the edge cases that automation can't catch.

Together, they close the loop. The checker keeps the everyday stuff out of the audit. The audit catches what the checker can't, and feeds that back into the corpus. That's the integration.

Close thank you

the whole idea

Accessibility should be how the code is written — not a thing you check at the end.

Every accessibility tool that came before this one assumed the code was already written. That's why the loop is so slow, and why the same issues keep coming back. We think the moment to get accessibility right is the moment the code is being written — whether a person writes it or an AI does.

If you want the code to arrive accessible, that's what a11y-checker is for.
